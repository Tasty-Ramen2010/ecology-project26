Chapter 01 · The City You Know

Cairo is a city of sun and stone — built on a desert, cradled by the Nile, and held in place by winds that have blown west-to-east for ten thousand years.

Today, Cairo sits at 30° north, squarely inside a band of dry subtropical air. Westerly winds roll in over North Africa carrying almost no moisture. The Mokattam Plateau to the east forces any stray air mass to descend, compress, and warm — a classic rain-shadow, and the reason the nearby desert is so extreme.

Seasons here come in two registers: a mild winter that hovers between 50 and 75°F, and a long, blunt summer that routinely breaks 95°F. Water is the Nile and the Nile alone — a green ribbon threading through an ochre world, feeding the canals, Lake Qarun, and the Wadi El Natrun basins on its way to the sea.

The engine behind the change.

Cairo doesn't just warm or cool — it is lifted. As the African plate drives north into Eurasia, a new mountain range rises along the suture. Cairo, now on the windward flank, steps into a cooler latitude, catches the rain, and watches its water map redraw itself.

01 · Tectonic Plates

Africa drives north into Eurasia.

Today Cairo sits on the northern edge of the African plate, with the Eurasian plate holding the Mediterranean between them. As the two plates continue to converge, they clash and buckle, lifting a new mountain range along the collision zone. Cairo rides the crust upward and northward, away from the equator and into cooler, less directly-lit latitudes.

02 · Wind & Rain

Cairo on the windward side.

The Westerlies now strike the new range on Cairo's side first. Air is forced upward, cools, and drops its moisture as rain and snow — the classic orographic wet flank. The descending side becomes the new rain-shadow desert, so the climate that currently belongs to Cairo migrates across the range.

03 · Bodies of Water

New rivers, new basins.

Reliable rainfall and spring-melt carve fresh drainage networks off the range. Valleys fill with alpine lakes and perennial streams; the old Nile is long gone, but the windward flank hosts an entirely new water map — the source of the temperate forest's stability.

04 · Soil Chemistry

Weathering loads the ground.

Extra moisture in air and soil accelerates geological weathering. Freshly exposed mountain minerals — phosphorus, potassium, calcium — break down into the soil column, fertilising it and opening a niche for broadleaf forest. Old desert specialists can't compete; new temperate lineages flourish.

Three migrations, one climate.

As Cairo tilts from hot desert to temperate upland, its living fauna sorts itself into three groups — those that leave, those that adapt in place, and those that walk in along the green front.

Emigrants · Desert specialists

The specialists leave.

Fennec Fox Vulpes zerda Spiny-tailed Lizard Uromastyx aegyptia Date Palm Phoenix dactylifera

These lineages are evolutionarily optimised for arid conditions and temperatures above 100°F. As tectonic movement carries Cairo into cooler, more temperate latitudes, heat-dependent organisms lose out in local competition. Over millions of years their populations drift toward the new equatorial zones — wherever their narrow niche still exists.

Drift · heat · narrow niche
Residents · Generalists

The generalists stay.

Striped Hyena Hyaena hyaena Egyptian Mongoose Herpestes ichneumon

The descendants of the striped hyena and Egyptian mongoose remain through sheer biological plasticity. Neither is tied to a single food source or a narrow temperature range. Rather than fleeing the cooling climate, they evolve alongside it — adjusting diet, hunting behaviour, and physiology to match the changing landscape.

Plasticity · wide diet · adapt in place
Immigrants · Forest pioneers

Temperate pioneers walk in.

Oak Quercus spp. Golden Eagle Aquila chrysaetos Red Fox Vulpes vulpes

As the region transforms into a cooler, greener biome, species adapted to stable, high-vegetation environments move in. The oak expands its range through seed dispersal as soils enrich and climate mildens. The golden eagle and red fox fill the predatory niches left behind, following an explosion of forest-dwelling prey replacing the old desert fauna.

Dispersal · new niches · rich soil

The forest's nutrient cycles.

In the Cairo of +250 million years, a temperate oak forest builds itself out of four overlapping loops — carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, and water. Each one pulls material from air, stone, or flesh and folds it into the living ground.

01 · Carbon

Oak forests as carbon sinks.

Massive oak stands pull CO₂ from the atmosphere and lock it into their wooden trunks. Fallen carbon settles into a thick O-horizon, where fungi and decomposers recycle it back into stable, lush soil.

02 · Nitrogen

Nitrogen hotspots from hyena & mongoose.

Waste and carcasses of striped hyenas and mongooses create local nitrogen hotspots. Soil bacteria convert it through biotic exchange into plant-usable forms, fertilising winter wheat and the forest floor.

03 · Phosphorus

Rain on new mountain stone.

Rain washes over the freshly risen mountains, releasing phosphorus-rich minerals through geological weathering. These nutrients flow directly into the forest, where root systems absorb them to fuel sustained growth.

04 · Water

An orographic transpiration loop.

Moist air rises over the highlands, cools, and falls as orographic rainfall. Oaks and understorey return moisture through transpiration, reinforcing the loop and stabilising Cairo's new water supply.

Two food webs, two Cairos.

Same latitude, same city name, separated by 250 million years of continental drift, mountain-building, and rainfall. The trophic levels hold — but the names of who eats whom change completely.
